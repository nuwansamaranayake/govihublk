# Integrated Pest Management for Sri Lanka's Dry Zone Farms

## Introduction

Pest management is one of the most critical challenges facing farmers in Sri Lanka's dry zone (Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, Kurunegala, and adjoining districts). The hot, seasonally dry climate creates conditions that favour rapid pest multiplication, particularly during the Yala season (April–September). Integrated Pest Management (IPM) offers a science-based framework that reduces chemical dependency while maintaining or improving crop yields.

## Key Pests of the Dry Zone

### Rice Pests

**Brown Planthopper (Nilaparvata lugens — BPH)**
BPH is the most destructive rice pest in Sri Lanka. Infestations cause "hopperburn" — circular patches of dead, brown plants. BPH thrives in dense canopies and high nitrogen conditions. Monitor fields weekly from tillering stage. Release Trichogramma egg parasitoids or use yellow sticky traps for early detection. Reduce nitrogen rates to discourage outbreaks.

**Gall Midge (Orseolia oryzae)**
This stem borer produces "silver shoots" — infested tillers that fail to produce panicles. Losses can exceed 30% in vulnerable varieties. Use resistant varieties such as BG 300 and practice synchronised planting at the district level to break the pest cycle.

**Leaf Folder (Cnaphalocrocis medinalis)**
Larvae fold leaves and feed on the internal tissue. Severe infestations reduce photosynthesis. Natural predators — spiders and hymenopteran parasitoids — are highly effective. Avoid unnecessary insecticide sprays that kill beneficial insects.

**Paddy Bug (Leptocorisa oratorius)**
These bugs pierce developing grains, causing "chalky" or unfilled grains at harvest. Populations spike at heading and grain-fill stages. Sweep net sampling twice per week is recommended. Action threshold: >5 bugs per 10 sweeps.

### Vegetable Pests (Chilli, Tomato, Brinjal)

**Thrips (Thrips palmi)**
Thrips cause silvery streaks on leaves and are vectors of Tomato spotted wilt virus. Blue sticky traps deployed at 50 per hectare provide effective monitoring. Neem-based sprays (azadirachtin 0.03%) applied fortnightly reduce populations without harming natural enemies.

**Whitefly (Bemisia tabaci)**
Whitefly transmits geminiviruses in chilli and tomato. Yellow sticky traps at plant height intercept adults. Reflective mulches disorient whiteflies. In severe cases, Spiromesifen or Buprofezin are registered for use in Sri Lanka.

**Fruit Borer (Leucinodes orbonalis) — Brinjal**
The most devastating brinjal pest. Larvae bore into fruits, making them unmarketable. Remove and destroy infested fruits twice a week. Pheromone traps (1 per 25 m) monitor adult populations. A threshold of 10 moths per trap per week signals the need for intervention.

## Biological Control

Sri Lanka's dry zone has a rich community of natural enemies:

- **Spiders**: The most abundant predators in rice, consuming BPH nymphs and adults.
- **Trichogramma chilonis**: An egg parasitoid available from the Department of Agriculture. Released at 50,000 per hectare at weekly intervals during vulnerable crop stages.
- **Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt)**: Effective against lepidopteran larvae. Use formulations with 32,000 IU/mg. Apply during early larval instars.
- **Entomopathogenic fungi (Beauveria bassiana)**: Effective against whitefly and thrips in vegetable crops.

## Cultural Control Practices

1. **Crop rotation**: Alternate rice with legumes (cowpea, green gram) to break pest cycles.
2. **Synchronised planting**: Coordinate planting dates within a one-week window at the farmer organisation level to reduce pest build-up.
3. **Field sanitation**: Remove crop residues and burn infested plant matter immediately after harvest.
4. **Optimal plant spacing**: Proper spacing (20×15 cm for rice) reduces humidity in the canopy and discourages hoppers.
5. **Trap crops**: Plant susceptible varieties on field borders to concentrate pests before targeted treatment.

## Chemical Control — Last Resort

When pest populations exceed economic thresholds, targeted chemical control may be necessary:

- **Always read the label** and use the minimum effective rate.
- Apply during morning hours (before 09:00) or late afternoon to protect bees.
- Rotate chemistry classes to prevent resistance development.
- Recommended chemicals registered in Sri Lanka: Cartap (BPH), Lambda-cyhalothrin (paddy bug), Trifloxystrobin (sheath blight), Imidacloprid (whitefly seed treatment).
- **Do not** spray pesticides within 500 m of water bodies, as runoff threatens irrigation canal biodiversity.

## Economic Thresholds for Key Pests

| Pest | Crop Stage | Action Threshold |
|------|-----------|-----------------|
| BPH | Tillering–heading | 10 nymphs per hill |
| Leaf folder | Vegetative | 25% leaf damage |
| Paddy bug | Heading | 5 bugs/10 sweeps |
| Fruit borer | All | 10 moths/trap/week |

## Record Keeping

Maintain a pest scouting diary recording: date, crop stage, pest species, population density, action taken, and cost. This data enables better decisions in future seasons and satisfies requirements for certification schemes such as GlobalG.A.P.
