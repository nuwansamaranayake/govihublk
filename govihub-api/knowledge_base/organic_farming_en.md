# Organic Farming Practices for Sri Lanka's Dry Zone

## Introduction

Organic farming is gaining rapid momentum among smallholder farmers in Sri Lanka's dry zone as a response to rising input costs, soil degradation, and growing consumer demand for chemical-free produce. The combination of traditional Sinhalese agricultural knowledge and modern organic science provides a powerful toolkit for sustainable productivity in districts such as Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, Kurunegala, and Vavuniya.

## Core Principles of Organic Farming

Organic farming rejects synthetic fertilisers, pesticides, and genetically modified organisms. Instead, it relies on:

1. **Soil health first** — healthy, biologically active soil grows healthy plants that are naturally more resistant to pests and diseases.
2. **Closed-loop nutrient cycling** — organic matter from farm outputs (compost, crop residues, manure) replaces nutrients removed by harvest.
3. **Biodiversity** — diverse plantings attract natural pest predators and pollinators, reducing the need for external inputs.
4. **Water conservation** — mulching and cover crops reduce evaporation, critical in the dry zone.

## Soil Building

### Compost Making

High-quality compost is the foundation of organic dry-zone farming. Follow these steps:

- **Collect materials**: Mix three parts carbon-rich material (dried paddy straw, coconut shells, dry leaves) with one part nitrogen-rich material (green weeds, kitchen waste, cattle dung).
- **Layer and moisten**: Build a pile 1.2 m high in layers, moistening each layer so it feels like a wrung-out sponge.
- **Turn weekly**: Aerate the pile every 7–10 days. In the dry zone's heat (35–40 °C), compost is typically ready in 45–60 days.
- **Application rate**: Apply 5–10 tonnes per hectare before land preparation.

### Vermicompost

Earthworm (Eisenia foetida) compost is richer in available nutrients than regular compost. Produce it in shaded pits using cattle dung + crop residues. Apply at 2–3 t/ha, focusing on incorporation in the root zone.

### Green Manures and Cover Crops

- **Gliricidia sepium (Gliricidia)**: A nitrogen-fixing tree widely grown in Sri Lanka. Lop branches and incorporate 5–10 t/ha of green biomass four weeks before planting. This can replace 50–75 kg/ha of urea.
- **Crotalaria juncea (Sunn Hemp)**: Fast-growing legume. Sow, grow for 60 days, then incorporate.
- **Mucuna pruriens (Velvet Bean)**: Excellent weed suppressor and nitrogen fixer, well-adapted to dry conditions.

## Organic Pest and Disease Management

### Neem-Based Sprays

Neem (Azadirachta indica) grows abundantly across Sri Lanka's dry zone. Prepare a homemade spray by:

1. Crushing 500 g of neem seeds and soaking in 10 litres of water overnight.
2. Straining through cloth.
3. Adding 5 ml of liquid soap as an emulsifier.
4. Spraying early morning at 7-day intervals.

Effective against: BPH, whitefly, leafhoppers, mites.

### Garlic and Chilli Extract

Mix 100 g crushed garlic + 50 g chilli powder in 5 litres of water. Strain and dilute 1:10. Repels aphids, thrips, and stem borers. Safe for pollinators when applied in early morning.

### Traps and Physical Barriers

- **Yellow sticky traps**: At 50 per hectare for whitefly and thrips monitoring.
- **Light traps**: UV traps attract nocturnal moths. Set at crop height from 18:00–22:00 during heading stage.
- **Kaolin clay spray**: A 5% solution acts as a physical barrier against fruit flies and weevils in vegetable crops.

## Water Management for Organic Farms

Water efficiency is paramount in the dry zone, where annual rainfall averages only 900–1 800 mm and is highly seasonal.

**Mulching** with dried paddy straw or coconut fronds at a 5–8 cm depth:
- Reduces soil surface temperature by up to 8 °C.
- Cuts irrigation requirement by 30–40%.
- Suppresses weeds, reducing labour costs.

**Drip irrigation** is increasingly cost-accessible for vegetable farmers through government subsidy schemes. Combines well with mulching for chilli, tomato, and cucumber.

**Rainwater harvesting**: Small farm ponds (Wewa-miniature) can store monsoon runoff for use during dry spells. A 10 × 10 × 2 m pond stores approximately 200,000 litres.

## Certification and Market Access

Organic certification opens premium markets in Colombo and for export. Key schemes available in Sri Lanka:

- **Sri Lanka Organic Certification (SL-OC)** — administered by the Export Development Board.
- **Participatory Guarantee Systems (PGS)** — community-based peer certification, lower cost, suitable for smallholders.
- **GlobalG.A.P.** — required for EU export markets.

The certification process typically takes 3 years (conversion period). During this time, use GoviHub's advisory service to track compliance requirements and record keeping.

## Economic Considerations

| Comparison | Conventional | Organic |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Input cost | High | Low–Medium |
| Labour cost | Medium | Higher |
| Market price premium | None | 20–50% |
| Soil health trend | Declining | Improving |
| Risk in drought | Moderate | Lower (mulching/SOM) |

Studies by the Hector Kobbekaduwa Agrarian Research Institute (HARTI) in 2022 found that certified organic vegetable farmers in the Dambulla region achieved net incomes 35% higher than conventional counterparts within five years of conversion.

1. Get your soil tested at the nearest Agrarian Services Centre.
2. Start a compost pit using farm waste — no-cost input.
3. Plant a Gliricidia boundary hedge this season.
4. Attend a PGS farmer group in your district.
